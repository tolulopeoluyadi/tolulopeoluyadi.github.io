Sample 01 Case IQ — The Before State
The System That Was Already Breaking

APL’s Office of Legal Affairs supports multiple sensitive service areas — Ethics & Compliance, EEO, and Audits & Compliance. Before Case IQ, every one of these services ran on the same fragile, manual infrastructure.

📩
Email-Driven Intake
Requests arrived via email, web forms, or phone calls with no central queue. Intake depended on whoever saw the message first. No confirmation, no ticket number, no audit trail.
📄
Spreadsheet Tracking
Case status lived in individual spreadsheets maintained by each team. Files were version-controlled manually, creating duplicate records, stale data, and no single source of truth.
🔒
No Confidentiality Controls
Sensitive EEO and ethics cases were stored alongside routine requests with no role-based access. No way to restrict visibility by case type, team, or individual — a serious defensibility risk.
🔄
Inconsistent Handoffs
When a case moved between people or teams, the handoff was informal — an email forward or verbal update. Context was lost, timelines slipped, and ownership was undocumented.
📈
Reporting Required Cleanup
Generating a volume report or cycle-time analysis meant manually reconciling spreadsheets across teams. Data was incomplete, inconsistently categorized, and weeks out of date.
😬
Requester Visibility = Zero
Anyone who filed a complaint had no way to check its status without emailing and waiting. This created a flood of “status check” follow-ups that consumed admin time and eroded trust.
⚠️ Why This Couldn’t Continue
Legal case management requires defensibility — the ability to prove, under scrutiny, that every case was handled consistently, documented completely, and routed to the right people at the right time.
The manual process couldn’t provide that. Every missing timestamp, every undocumented handoff, every case that sat in someone’s inbox without a status update was a compliance exposure.
The organization needed a system, but the real challenge was designing one that worked across three different service areas with different workflows, different confidentiality requirements, and different stakeholder expectations — and deploying it all at once without breaking anyone’s active caseload.
Sample 01 Case IQ — My Role & Design Thinking
The Decisions I Made & Why They Were Hard

I serve as the primary liaison between APL’s Office of Legal Affairs and IT. My job is to translate competing stakeholder needs into a system that works — and deploy it across all three service areas simultaneously without disrupting active caseloads.

Design Tradeoffs I Navigated
Decision The Tension How I Resolved It
Shared vs. separate status models E&C, EEO, and A&C each have different workflows, timelines, and vocabulary. A single status model would flatten important distinctions; three separate models would kill cross-service reporting. Designed a shared status + team-specific sub-status architecture. Top-level statuses are universal (for reporting); sub-statuses capture the nuance each team needs.
Transparency vs. confidentiality Requesters want to see their case status in real time. But EEO and Ethics cases involve sensitive allegations where oversharing can compromise the investigation. Built role-based visibility rules at the case-type level. Requesters see status and next-step guidance; internal investigation details remain restricted to assigned team members.
Structured fields vs. adoption friction Reporting-quality data requires required fields and controlled vocabulary at intake. But every required field is friction that risks admins reverting to email. Identified the minimum viable field set for defensibility (timestamps, case type, assigned owner, status) and made everything else optional-but-encouraged.
Simultaneous rollout across 3 services Deploying to all three service areas at once meant configuring three different workflow variants, training three different teams, and migrating active cases — all without a “quiet” pilot period. Built a parallel operation window: ran old process and Case IQ side-by-side for 3 weeks so teams could build confidence while active cases stayed safe. Prioritized visible quick wins first.
Automated comms vs. personal touch Templated status-change emails scale consistency but risk feeling impersonal for sensitive cases where the requester is anxious or distrustful of the process. Created tiered communication templates: automated for routine updates; human-triggered with editable body text for sensitive milestones.
📌 How I Managed the Transition
Stakeholder mapping: identified power users, skeptics, and champions within each service area before configuration — tailored training accordingly
3-week parallel operation: old process and Case IQ ran side-by-side so teams built confidence without risk of dropping active cases
Weekly feedback cadence: 15-minute check-ins focused on “what’s confusing” and “what’s slower than before” — not feature requests
Quick wins first: prioritized the most visible pain point (status-check emails) so users saw immediate value before confronting the learning curve
📊 What I’m Measuring & Why
Cycle time by stage: how long cases sit in each status — to find specific bottlenecks, not just averages
Off-system case rate: how many cases are still tracked outside the system. This is the real adoption signal — not login counts
“Status check” email volume: if requesters are still emailing to ask “where’s my case?”, the visibility features need redesign
Data completeness trajectory: not a point-in-time score, but a trendline — are required fields getting filled more consistently week over week?
Sample 02 Janney Grants Evaluation
Measuring 40 Years of Staff-Driven Innovation
📋 The Challenge
Janney Grants have funded staff-driven innovation and thought leadership at JHU APL since 1984
No formal assessment of outcomes or organizational impact had ever been conducted
Goal: produce actionable insight for leadership — what outcomes are generated, where value concentrates, and how to strengthen the program
🧩 Why Measurement Is Hard
Diverse outputs: publications, conferences, international collaborations, downstream work
Multi-dimensional impact: visibility, knowledge exchange, partnerships — not just counts
Attribution gap: Janney can be a catalyst, but outcomes appear months or years later
🔬 Mixed-Methods Evaluation Design
Quantitative
Bibliometrics, citation indicators, output counts, structured program metrics
Qualitative
Surveys, interviews, focus groups capturing collaboration, visibility, and institutional benefits
Case Analyses
Deeper looks at representative activities: funding → outputs → outcomes pathways

Sample 03 Voyager Healthcare Research
Technology, Trust & Access Across Two Countries
🌱 Why This Research — The Personal Origin
During a serious health period in my family, digital tools meant to support care coordination added friction instead. The portal was confusing, information was hard to interpret, and the next step was never clear. When someone is managing fear, fatigue, and time pressure, unclear digital experiences don’t feel like minor inconvenience — they shape what people follow through on. That experience made me care about technology as a lived experience, not just a feature set. As an Obama-Chesky Voyager Scholar, I turned that question into research: why do some healthcare technologies get adopted and trusted while others — even free, well-funded ones — sit unused?
🇯🇵
Japan
Aging Population · Assistive Robotics · Telemedicine

Japan faces the world’s most advanced aging crisis: over 29% of the population is 65+, the caregiver workforce is shrinking, and the government has invested billions in assistive robotics, remote monitoring, and telemedicine.

I studied how elderly patients and caregivers actually experience these tools — visiting care facilities, observing robotic-assist interactions, and interviewing patients managing chronic disease via telemedicine.

Key tension: Japan has the infrastructure and the government support, but adoption among elderly patients remains uneven. The barriers are human — digital literacy, cognitive decline, distrust of non-face-to-face care, and the cultural expectation that healthcare is relational.

🇳🇬
Nigeria
Rural Access · Telemedicine · Infrastructure Constraints

Nigeria has approximately 1 doctor per 2,500+ people, with most specialists concentrated in Lagos and Abuja. For rural patients, getting a diagnosis can mean a full day of travel and a cost many families cannot absorb.

I researched how telemedicine platforms and mobile health tools extend care to underserved populations — interviewing clinicians, patients, health workers, and the teams building these products.

Key tension: The need is urgent and the potential enormous, but adoption depends on factors no one markets: data costs (patients avoid video calls to save bandwidth), device sharing (privacy on a family phone), and trust in a provider you’ve never met.

🔍 Research Methodology
Field Observation
Embedded in clinics, hospitals, care facilities, and community health centers. Observed real technology use in situ — not demos, not staged settings.
Stakeholder Interviews
Semi-structured interviews with clinicians, patients, caregivers, administrators, community health workers, and technology vendors.
Comparative Synthesis
Cross-context analysis separating universal adoption factors (trust, workflow-fit) from context-dependent ones (infrastructure, cost, regulation).
Design Translation
Findings synthesized into testable product opportunities and implementation frameworks — moving from academic insight to actionable design.
Core Findings
Finding 01
Trust Is the Feature — Not the Add-On
In both countries, adoption correlated most strongly with whether the patient could identify and verify the provider, understand what would happen with their data, and trust that follow-up would occur. In Nigeria, patients asked “who is this doctor?” before engaging; in Japan, elderly patients preferred tools their in-person doctor explicitly endorsed.
Finding 02
Workflow-Fit Determines Survival
Technologies that matched existing routines were adopted. Those requiring new behaviors, extra documentation, or parallel systems were abandoned — regardless of sophistication. A clinic in Nigeria stopped using a platform because it doubled charting time. A care facility in Japan shelved a monitoring tool because it required input from already-stretched staff.
Finding 03
Access = Friction + Affordability, Not Connectivity
In Nigeria, data costs actively shaped usage: patients avoided video consultations to save bandwidth, defaulting to text-based interactions that lost diagnostic richness. In Japan, elderly patients struggled with interface complexity on modern devices. “Access” is a design problem involving cost, literacy, device constraints, and cognitive load.
Finding 04
Human Support Loops Are Non-Negotiable
In every high-adoption case, there was a person — a community health worker, a family member, a clinic navigator — who helped the patient use the tool. Technology without “someone to ask” consistently failed at the last mile. The human loop isn’t a workaround; it’s part of the product.
Finding 05
Equity Can Be Designed In From Day One
Products designed for the most constrained users — low literacy, low bandwidth, older devices, shared phones — were more scalable and trusted by all user groups. A telemedicine tool in Nigeria designed for 2G networks worked better for urban users too. Designing for the edge case first improved the product for everyone.
Finding 06
Infrastructure Redefines “Usable”
A tool that works on stable Wi-Fi fails where power is intermittent or devices are shared. In Japan, even with strong infrastructure, reliability expectations among elderly users were extremely high — a single crash eroded willingness to try again. The minimum viable experience is set by the user’s worst-case environment.
Sample 03 From Insights to Direction
Two Testable Product Opportunities

The research pointed to two product-level opportunities grounded in what I observed — each translating a core finding into a design direction worth testing.

Opportunity 01
Trust & Continuity Layer
A lightweight layer built into telemedicine tools that addresses the #1 adoption blocker: trust. Verified provider identity, plain-language visit summaries, follow-up reminders, and low-bandwidth fallback — so the experience doesn’t break when connectivity does. Measure: completion rate, follow-up adherence, and patient trust.
Opportunity 02
Implementation Playbook
A structured onboarding kit for clinics adopting health tech: workflow mapping, staff training scripts, patient FAQ templates, and a first-week metrics dashboard. The insight: technology fails at the last mile without “someone to ask.” The playbook systematizes that human support loop. Measure: adoption rate with vs. without the playbook.
